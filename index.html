Pictured Rocks National Lakeshore, America’s first National Lakeshore, is along the southern shore of Lake Superior in Michigan’s beautiful Upper Peninsula. With stunning beaches, 10 inland lakes, and nearly 100 miles of trails, everyone can find something to enjoy at Pictured Rocks!
No matter the time of year, there is something to do at Pictured Rocks! Summer: visit beaches, backpack, and camp (permit required). Looking for views of the cliffs? The best way to see the cliffs is from the water on a commercial boat tour. Fall: Autumn colors in the Upper Peninsula are often breathtaking, drive the park roads to view the changing leaves! Hunting and fishing opportunities (state regulations apply) abound during fall! Winter: Cold weather brings otherworldly frozen waterfalls, called “curtains”. Snowshoeing, skiing, and ice fishing are other popular winter activities. Check road closures and conditions before heading into the park in the winter. Spring: Go birding or walk the marsh trail!

Yellowstone is the first national park in the United States. It covers over 2.2 million acres, and provides an opportunity to see wildlife and explore geothermal areas. In fact, Yellowstone contains about half the world's active geysers.
These unique opportunities also bring out a lot of bad decisions among the tourists. Every year visitors injure themselves or the wildlife by getting close to the animals. You can see examples of people making bad decisions by visiting Yellowstone National Park: Invasion of the Idiots, Tourons of Yellowstone, or Cowboy State Daily.

Sequoia National Park is a large forest located in the Sierra Nevada mountain range in California. It is famous for its gigantic sequoia and redwood trees, as well as its underground Crystal Cave. The park is a well-known attraction to many tourists, receiving more than a million visitors annually. In addition to its collection of giant trees, Sequoia National Park is also home to thousands of prevalent flora and fauna.
Perhaps the most famous feature of Sequoia National Park is the General Sherman tree – the largest tree in the world. Measuring in at 275 feet tall, the tree towers over the surrounding landscape and the people who come to view it. As of today, the General Sherman tree is estimated to be around 2200 years old.

Beyond its natural splendor, Acadia National Park is home to an array of wildlife, from peregrine falcons soaring above the cliffs to elusive white-tailed deer roaming the forests. Visitors can also explore the historic Jordan Pond House, a charming tea house overlooking Jordan Pond, where they can indulge in the park's famous popovers while surrounded by the serenity of nature. Whether scaling the heights of Cadillac Mountain or enjoying a leisurely day by the sea, Acadia National Park captivates all who venture into its embrace, offering an enriching experience that combines the tranquility of nature with a deep sense of historical and ecological significance.
Acadia National Park, nestled along the rugged coastline of Maine, stands as a testament to the stunning beauty of the northeastern United States. Established in 1916, Acadia is celebrated as the first national park east of the Mississippi River. The park covers over 49,000 acres, encompassing a diverse landscape that includes granite peaks, lush forests, pristine lakes, and the iconic rocky shores of Mount Desert Island. Acadia's crown jewel is Cadillac Mountain, the tallest peak on the U.S. Atlantic coast, offering visitors a breathtaking panoramic view of the sunrise that is among the first to touch the continental United States. The park's unique blend of mountainous terrain and coastal scenery provides an extraordinary setting for a variety of outdoor activities, from hiking and biking on an extensive network of trails to exploring the tranquil carriage roads designed by John D. Rockefeller Jr.
